---
title: Cue-to-cue mode
summary: Drill just your lines and their feeds — the rest collapses out of the way.
date: 2026-07-08
---

In a cue-to-cue rehearsal you only see the line (or lines) that cue into each of your
speeches, then your speech itself. Everything in between disappears — you stay focused on
listening for your trigger and responding.

> **Tip:** The same cue-to-cue lines can be pre-selected for recording or teaching, via the
> "Cue-to-cue only" checkbox on Selftape's and Timers' "choose how to proceed" dialogs — see
> the Selftape mode article.

## Turning it on

- Open the three-dot menu (top right) in Practice or Rehearse.
- Tap "Cue-to-cue mode". A tick appears and the scene is immediately filtered.
- Tap again — or leave the screen — to go back to the full script. Either way the view stays
  put on the line you were reading, rather than jumping to the top.

## What you see

- Each of your lines, with the line that feeds into it shown directly above.
- Everything else is folded into a grey "Skip - Cue-to-cue" card so you know how much context
  you're jumping over. It's separate from a "Skip - line hide" card (lines you've individually
  marked to skip in the Scene Editor) and a "Skip - manual" card (a placeholder someone typed
  in directly) — all three look similar but mean different things.
- Run, score and voice-commands all work exactly as normal — only the view is filtered.

> **Tip:** In Practice, tapping a "Skip - line hide" card previews the lines it's hiding and
> lets you Unskip All in one go, without leaving the screen. Cue-to-cue and manual cards aren't
> tappable — there's nothing behind them to restore.

## Short feeds and fast exchanges

- If the feed line is very short (e.g. "Yes." or "What?") the app automatically includes one
  extra line above it, so you have enough context to anchor your response.
- When several short lines fly back and forth and you're one of the speakers — a rapid exchange
  — the whole burst is shown together rather than being broken up. The rhythm of the exchange
  is itself the memory device. A fast exchange between other characters that you're not part of
  is still folded away as normal.

## Sections and speeches

- A section or speech's start and end markers always stay visible under cue-to-cue, even if the
  boundary line itself would normally be folded away — so brackets, chips and "Run section N"
  keep working. Only the lines inside the section are filtered as normal.
- You can't start defining a new section or speech while cue-to-cue is on — the menu shows a
  warning with a one-tap option to turn cue-to-cue off first. Dragging or deleting an existing
  section/speech is unaffected.

## Stage-direction pauses

- A stage direction (e.g. "(pause)" or "(long silence)") can be given an optional pause time in
  the editor, just like a line's Speak Time. It shows a small clock icon when a time is set.
- In Practice and Rehearsal, the run goes quiet for that many seconds before the next cue — it
  isn't shown as a countdown and it's never scored.
- If cue-to-cue has folded that stretch of the scene away, the direction's pause is skipped
  too, since it never plays for you in the filtered view.

> **Tip:** Cue-to-cue is great once you know the shape of a scene and want to stress-test just
> the moments where you need to pick up your cue. For building the shape in the first place,
> run the full scene.
