Actors have argued about the "right" way to learn lines for as long as there have
been lines. A few methods keep winning in both rehearsal rooms and memory research.

## Retrieval beats re-reading

Reading your script again *feels* productive, but memory research is blunt: you
remember what you **retrieve**, not what you re-read. Hiding your lines and forcing
yourself to produce them — then checking — is the single biggest upgrade you can
make. This is exactly what Practice mode's hide-and-reveal is for.

## Cues, not paragraphs

Your brain doesn't store a monologue as a wall of text; it stores *this line follows
that cue*. Rehearsing cue-to-response pairs (cue-to-cue mode) builds the links you
actually need on stage.

## Little and often

Two twenty-minute sessions beat one two-hour slog. Spacing forces your memory to do
a little forgetting and re-finding, which is what makes it stick. The accuracy
heat-map makes a perfect five-minute session: pick the reddest band, run it, done.

## Say it out loud

Silent reading recruits a fraction of the memory you'll use in performance. Speaking
engages your voice, breath and hearing — the state you'll actually be in. Rehearse
mode's speech scoring keeps you honest.
